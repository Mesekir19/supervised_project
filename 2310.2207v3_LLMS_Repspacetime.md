Report: Spatiotemporal Representations in Large Language Models

1. Executive Introduction: The "Stochastic Parrot" vs. "World Model" Debate

A foundational conflict in artificial intelligence research is the nature of the representations formed by Large Language Models (LLMs). Are these systems merely "stochastic parrots" that excel at superficial statistical correlations, or do they develop coherent, grounded World Models? We define a world model as a compact, interpretable representation of the generative process underlying training data.

In a striking discovery, researchers have demonstrated that LLMs—trained exclusively on the objective of next-token prediction—spontaneously organize factual information into sophisticated internal maps of space and time. This study finds that models like Llama-2 do not simply memorize strings of text; they learn rich, linear representations of the physical world and historical timelines across multiple scales, from global coordinates to the granularity of New York City neighborhoods.

2. Methodology: How to Extract a Map from a Neural Network

To visualize the "mind’s eye" of these models, researchers utilized linear probes. A linear probe is a simple ridge regression model fitted to the model's internal activations (A) to predict a target (Y), such as latitude/longitude or a year. The probe follows the standard ridge regression formula:

Ŵ = (A^TA + \lambda I)^{-1}A^TY

By applying these probes to the hidden states of Llama-2 and Pythia, researchers tested the Linearity Hypothesis: the idea that if a simple linear model can extract complex coordinates, the model itself must have organized that information linearly. The researchers utilized six primary datasets:

Dataset	Category	Entity Count	Examples
World	Space	39,585	"Los Angeles", "Caspian Sea", "Canary Islands"
USA	Space	29,997	"Fenway Park", "Columbia University", "Riverside County"
NYC	Space	19,838	"Borden Avenue Bridge", "Trump International Hotel"
Figures	Time	37,539	"Cleopatra", "Dante Alighieri", "Carl Sagan"

A technical nuance discovered during probing was that performance on the Headlines dataset improved significantly when probing on the "period token" following a headline. This suggests that the model uses periods as "summary containers" to consolidate the temporal information of the preceding sentence.

3. Evidence for Internal Spatial and Temporal Maps

The probing experiments provided robust evidence that LLMs construct internal spatiotemporal geometries.

* Scale and Performance: Quality of representation scales with both parameter count and training data. Llama-2 models (trained on 2T tokens) significantly outperformed the Pythia family (trained on 300B tokens). While the NYC dataset was the most challenging due to entity obscurity, Llama-2-70B showed the highest relative gains, suggesting that larger models develop increasingly granular "meshes" of reality.
* Layer Depth: Maps are not present in initial layers. Representation quality increases steadily through the first half of the model, reaching a plateau at approximately the halfway point of the model's depth.
* The Linearity Hypothesis: Researchers found that highly expressive nonlinear MLP probes provided negligible improvements over linear ones. This synthesis is critical: it indicates that the internal "geometry" of the model is fundamentally linear. The probe is not "learning" the map; it is simply uncovering a structure the model has already organized.
* Precision: Precision was measured using Spearman rank correlation (\rho). The 70B model achieved remarkable accuracy across domains: Art release dates (\rho = 0.925), Historical Figures (\rho = 0.921), and NYT Headlines (\rho = 0.863).

4. The Discovery of "Space" and "Time" Neurons

Beyond aggregate layer-wise representations, researchers identified individual Space Neurons and Time Neurons. While most features in LLMs are likely distributed in Superposition—meaning many neurons work together to represent one concept—the existence of these "monosemantic" neurons serves as a lower bound for the model’s representational capacity.

* Latitude Neurons: Units such as L20 N7573 in Llama-2-7b exhibit activation levels that correlate directly with the north-south position of an entity.
* Time Neurons: Unit L19 N3610 was identified as a dedicated temporal encoder for art and entertainment.
* Causal Interventions: To prove these neurons aren't just "bystander" correlations, researchers performed causal interventions. By "pinning" the activation of neuron L19.3610, they could force the model to change its factual output. For instance, manually adjusting this single neuron's activation shifted the model's prediction of a song’s release decade from the 1970s to the 1990s.

5. Robustness and Generalization Checks

To verify these maps were generalized world models rather than memorized lookup tables, several checks were performed:

* Relative vs. Absolute Positioning: In "Block Holdout" tests (where entire countries or centuries were removed from the probe's training set), the probes could still predict the relative positions of entities within the held-out block. This suggests the model knows the internal geometry (relative position), while the probe's task is primarily to learn the "transformation" from model coordinates to absolute human coordinates.
* Unified Representations: The representations are unified, meaning the model uses the same internal map for different entity types (e.g., the same coordinate system for a city as for a natural landmark).
* PCA Findings: Principal Component Analysis (PCA) revealed that space and time are significant enough to be among the top dozen components of the model's data variance. Notably, the Spearman correlation (\rho) increases much more rapidly than R^2 as the number of PCA components (k) increases. This implies that the model masters the "rank order" (e.g., knowing that London is north of Paris) with very few dimensions, even before it can pinpoint absolute coordinates.

6. Significance for the Field of AI

The discovery of structured spatiotemporal representations has profound implications:

1. Interpretability: We are moving toward a "mechanistic" understanding of LLMs, proving that factual recall is organized geometrically within the residual stream.
2. Reliability & Safety: Mapping the "internal world" of an AI is a prerequisite for identifying where its world model is brittle, which is critical for mitigating hallucinations and ensuring reasoning robustness.
3. Future Architecture: These findings suggest that future models might benefit from explicit hierarchical meshes—coordinate systems that grow in resolution as the model scales.
4. Biological Parallel: The researchers noted that these artificial neurons function similarly to place and grid cells found in the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex of biological brains. This suggests that both artificial and biological intelligences may converge on the same mathematical solutions for navigating the world.
