# Literature Review: GeoLLM (ICLR 2024)

## 1. Research Question
The main goal of this research was to find out if the vast amount of knowledge "compressed" inside Large Language Models (LLMs) during their pre-training can be used for geospatial prediction tasks. The authors specifically wanted to see if LLMs could predict indicators like population density, asset wealth, or household income as effectively as traditional methods like satellite imagery.

## 2. Methodology: How They Tackled the Problem
The researchers found that **zero-shot prompting** (simply asking the model a question without training it first) is not effective if you only use geographic coordinates. To solve this, they developed the **GeoLLM** method.

### A. The "Smart" Prompt Design
Instead of using only latitude and longitude, they utilized **OpenStreetMap (OSM)** data to create a detailed text-based prompt.
* **Reverse-Geocoding:** They used the *Nominatim* tool to turn coordinates into a full address (neighborhood, city, county, and country).
* **Nearby Places:** They used the *Overpass API* to identify the 10 closest locations within a 100km radius. For each place, the prompt included its name, distance, and cardinal direction (e.g., "0.7 km North: Columbus Circle").
* **Task Context:** The prompt ended with a clear task name and a scale for the prediction (e.g., "0.0 to 9.9").

### B. Fine-Tuning the Models
The team didn't just design a prompt; they fine-tuned the models to connect their internal weights to these specific tasks.
* **Models Used:** They compared GPT-3.5, Llama 2 (7B), and RoBERTa.
* **The Process:** For Llama 2, they used **QLoRA** (4-bit quantization) to make the training efficient, training only 33 million parameters.
* **Text-to-Prediction:** They treated the task as a classification problem rather than regression. They converted numerical labels into 100 "bins" (0.0 to 9.9) so the model could "write" the answer as text.

## 3. Key Achievements and Results
* **Performance Boost:** The GeoLLM method showed a **70% improvement** in accuracy (measured by Pearson's $r^2$) compared to basic baselines[.
* **Outperforming Satellites:** GPT-3.5 often equaled or exceeded the performance of satellite-based benchmarks for predicting wealth and population.
* **Sample Efficiency:** The models were very efficient, meaning they could make good predictions even with small amounts of training data (as few as 100 samples).
* **Global Robustness:** The method worked consistently well across different continents and was not limited to just one region.

## 4. Conclusions
The authors concluded that LLMs are effectively **geospatial foundation models**.hey contain a "hidden map" of the world that can be unlocked with the right prompting strategy. This research is important because it offers a new, cheaper way to gather social and economic data in areas where satellite imagery might be too expensive or lack predictive power.

## 5. Personal Reflection (What I Learned)
This paper taught me that **prompt engineering** is not just about writing a good question; it is about providing the right context to "bridge" raw data (like coordinates) with the model's internal knowledge. I also learned that "text" (addresses and landmarks) can be a more powerful covariate for socio-economic tasks than "visuals" (satellite photos). Finally, seeing how **QLoRA** allowed the researchers to fine-tune a massive model like Llama 2 on a single GPU was very inspiring.
