# Literature Review: GeoLLM (ICLR 2024)

## 1. Research Question
The main goal of this research was to find out if the vast amount of knowledge "compressed" inside Large Language Models (LLMs) during their pre-training can be used for geospatial prediction tasks. The authors specifically wanted to see if LLMs could predict indicators like population density, asset wealth, or household income as effectively as traditional methods like satellite imagery.

## 2. Methodology: How They Tackled the Problem
The researchers found that **zero-shot prompting** (simply asking the model a question without training it first) is not effective if you only use geographic coordinates. To solve this, they developed the **GeoLLM** method.

### A. The "Smart" Prompt Design
The authors argue that raw coordinates are a poor interface for LLMs. To fix this, they used OSM data to translate numbers into "spatial context" that the model can understand.

* **Data Extraction Tools:** They used **Nominatim** for reverse-geocoding (turning coordinates into addresses) and the **Overpass API** to find nearby Points of Interest (POIs).
* **Prompt Construction:** The OSM data was used to build a structured prompt:
    * **Address:** A detailed description from the neighborhood level to the country name.
    * **Nearby Places:** A list of the 10 closest locations within 100km, including their names, distances, and directions (e.g., "0.7 km North: Columbus Circle").
* **Result:** This design allows the LLM to "look up" specific information stored in its weights about those named locations.

### B. Fine-Tuning the Models
The paper provides a clear framework for fine-tuning LLMs for these tasks:

* **Task Formulation:** They treated the prediction as a **classification task** rather than regression because LLMs work better with tokens. They scaled the ground-truth values into 100 bins ranging from **0.0 to 9.9**.
* **Fine-Tuning Strategy:**
    * **GPT-3.5:** Fine-tuned via the OpenAI API using 2–4 epochs depending on the sample size.
    * **Llama 2 (7B):** Fine-tuned using **QLoRA** (4-bit quantization), which is very efficient—it only requires training 33 million parameters.
    * **Objective:** They used **unsupervised learning** on the concatenated prompt and label, meaning the model learned to predict the "answer" as the next set of tokens in a sequence.
* **Sample Efficiency:** The models achieved high performance even with only **100 to 1,000 training samples**, proving you don't need a massive dataset to get results.

## 3. Key Achievements and Results
* **Performance Boost:** The GeoLLM method showed a **70% improvement** in accuracy (measured by Pearson's $r^2$) compared to basic baselines[.
* **Outperforming Satellites:** GPT-3.5 often equaled or exceeded the performance of satellite-based benchmarks for predicting wealth and population.
* **Sample Efficiency:** The models were very efficient, meaning they could make good predictions even with small amounts of training data (as few as 100 samples).
* **Global Robustness:** The method worked consistently well across different continents and was not limited to just one region.
* **Scaling:** Performance improved as the model size increased, with GPT-3.5 outperforming Llama 2 by 19%.

## 4. Conclusions
The authors conclude that LLMs are **geospatial foundation models**. By using OSM data to "prompt" the model correctly and fine-tuning it with QLoRA, researchers can extract high-resolution geographic data that was previously hidden in the model's weights.

## 5. Personal Reflection (What I Learned)
I learned that we can turn a "map problem" into a "language problem" by using **OSM as a bridge**. The paper shows that **QLoRA** is a practical way for us to fine-tune large models on specific datasets without needing a supercomputer. Most importantly, it proves that the **structure of the prompt** (adding nearby places and addresses) is the key to unlocking the model's "memory" of the physical world.
This paper taught me that **prompt engineering** is not just about writing a good question; it is about providing the right context to "bridge" raw data (like coordinates) with the model's internal knowledge. I also learned that "text" (addresses and landmarks) can be a more powerful covariate for socio-economic tasks than "visuals" (satellite photos). Finally, seeing how **QLoRA** allowed the researchers to fine-tune a massive model like Llama 2 on a single GPU was very inspiring.
