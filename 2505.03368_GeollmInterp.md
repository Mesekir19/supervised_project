# Geospatial Mechanistic Interpretability of Large Language Models

**Paper ID:** 2505.03368

**Authors:** Stef De Sabbata, Stefano Mizzaro, Kevin Roitero

**Paper Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.03368



## Introduction

LLMs (Large Language Models) have demonstrated increasingly capabilities across a multitude of of NLP tasks. Of particular interest to this papers research was the increasing study and use of LLMs in the field of geography, as there is a lack of knowledge in how LLMs function internally in that regard. However, the current investigation method called linear probing has limited ability to properly probe geographical relationships. As such, this paper proposes a new framework for investigating LLM's internal geographical workings and investigates its performance in comparison.

## Motivations

--- Investigate the performance of a new framework for investigating the inner workings of how LLMs handle geospatial information.

--- Gain a deeper understanding of the mechanics of geospatial representation in LLMs


## Model and Data

**Model Investigated:** Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2
    
**Location Data:** GeoNames Free Gazetteer Data repository


## Probing

Probing in this paper refers specifically to techniques which extract and analyze the activations of a model while a prompt is being processed, something that is acknowledged as not being the only meaning of the term in the context of neural model investigation. According to the authors, a key aspect of probing in regards to LLMs is that probing techniques are non-intrusive and architecture agnostic, thus not requiring any additional fine tuning or training in order to investigate model internals.

One particular method or probing -and the one used in this paper- is the creation and used of target prompts to assess, capture, and judge a models knowledge and internals. 



### Methodology


From *GeoNames* placenames from three countries are sourced to be fed into Mistral for analysis, each corresponding to areas with broadly similar population sizes. For each country the placename entered into the model for analysis is entered as a prompt rendered as follows:

    -- By Country
        -- United States in format: [placename], [state]
            -- 4090 Place names in four states
        -- United Kingdom in format: [placename], [country]
            -- 6294 Place Names
            -- Where country coressponds to either England, Scotland, Wales, and etc
        -- Italy in format: [placename], [province]
            -- 9959 Place Names
            
The motivation in including state, country, and province is to reduce ambiguity as it allows differentiation between places that share a name but are in different areas and reinforces that the name refers to a location rather than a person or organization.

From *Mistral* 4,096 post-attention normalized activations of the transformer layers 7, 15, and 31 (with layers starting at 0) were extracted to obtain an understanding of internal representation at different layers. In this internal representation according to the paper layer 7 corresponds to early token-level patterns; Layer 15 corresponds to middle level contextual patterns; and Layer 31 corresponds to late stage high level abstractions. 

At each layer the results of feeding each placename prompt are captured and joined with the locations geographical coordinates (in latitude and longitude) to calculate global and local Moran's I to measure the spatial autocorrelation (thus determining geospatial relation). 

The expected results from this are that areas from within the same area should have similar values with the degree of complexity in spatial relations observed depending on how the model's training captured geographical and areas conceptually in the weights.
        

### Results

In analysis through probing high autocorrelation was seen at the level of state, country, and province mentioned in the prompt. Additionally, with the Italian prompts in particular a clear pattern of correlation regarding north, south and central regions appeared despite not being factored into the prompt, hinting that broader regions and geographical areas may be encoded as part of the internal representation. Additionally several neurons in layer 15 have high autocorrelation scores linking them to particular regions suggesting some contextual complexity in regards to regional encoding. However these highly autocorrelated neurons don't only react to a singular region or area which the authors argue is evidence that something they call the superposition hypothesis might hold, a topic the authors hold off on discussing until introducing their framework.

## Mechanistic interpretability

The framework argued by the authors is called Mechanistic interpretability, which seeks to analyze how models encode and process information by breaking down internal representation into smaller more easy to interpret features. A key concept of which is the superposition hypothesis which suggests that in order to represent features greater than the number of network neurons, multiple features will be encoded by sharing between or overlapping over multiple neurons. A factor which explains difficulties that can occur when trying to interpret individual neurons

### Methodology

In order to extract interpretable features from the model, sparse autoencoders are combined with spatial autocorrelation to deconstruct the model's neuron activations into simpler interpretable features and map the internal relations into geographic space. The autoencoders are trained based on the activation of the layers in response to prompts much like with probing with the particular implementation based off previous efforts and proposals from Templeton et al (Scaling Monosemanticity: Extracting Interpretable Features from Claude 3 Sonnet.) and Gao et al (Scaling and evaluating sparse autoencoders; 2024.). After the features were extracted the Moran's I was calculated and evaluated at a global and local level as previously.

### Results

The analysis showed that with the Mechanistic Interpretability method particular regions, areas, and/or locations both mentioned in the prompt and otherwise are identifiable with singular features. With there being features that encode for particular areas of Italy such as south or north-east as well as with concepts such as 'urban' showing high autocorrelation with large cities such as New York, London, or Milano. However, according the the autocorrelation results only 67 features of the 32,768 extracted had statistically significant spatial autocorrelation. This is argued to indicate that while models may encode some geographical information it is diffuse and likely further superimposed with non geographical concepts, although that there is significant spatial autocorrelation to begin with suggest that LLM's can indeed capture this information.



## Conclusions

--- Geographical information seems to be distributed across the network rather than to individual neurons.

--- The distributed nature of internal representations increases the difficulties encountered in investigating and interpreting a model's internal connections between geographical concepts and locations

--- Sparse Encoders when combined spatial analysis appears to be capable of making better interpretable the internal geographical representations encoded in LLMs

---While bearing much potential, there are still many unanswered questions in regards geospatial reasoning in LLMs
